Hurricane and Typhoon Data Analysis (1851–2014)¶

Introduction¶

Tropical cyclones, including hurricanes and typhoons, are among the most impactful extreme weather events, affecting both human activities and natural systems.

This project aims to explore historical cyclone data from 1851 to 2014 in order to identify patterns in key atmospheric variables such as wind speed and pressure.

The analysis focuses on transforming raw data into meaningful insights through data cleaning, exploratory data analysis (EDA), and basic statistical interpretation.

  1. Data Loading

The dataset was imported and initially inspected to understand its structure, variables, and overall size. This step is essential to ensure familiarity with the data before performing any transformations or analysis.

  1. Removing the outliers and plotting new boxplots to see the results

    • Using Interquartile Method
In [99]:
Q1 = binarydata["Maximum Wind"].quantile(0.25)

Q3 = binarydata["Maximum Wind"].quantile(0.75)

IQR = Q3 - Q1

lower_bound= Q1-1.5*IQR

upper_bound= Q3+1.5*IQR

new_cleanData= binarydata[(binarydata["High_Wind_Flag"] >= lower_bound) & (binarydata["High_Wind_Flag"] <= upper_bound)]

plt.figure (figsize = (8,6))
sns.boxplot (x = binarydata ["High_Wind_Flag"], y = binarydata ["Maximum Wind"], hue="High_Wind_Flag", data=new_cleanData, palette=["skyblue", "lightcoral"])
plt.title ("Data Amputation (Removing Outliers) on Maximum Wind")
plt.xlabel ("High Wind Flag (0=low, 1=high)")
plt.ylabel ("Maximum Wind")
plt.show()
About the result:¶

After removing outliers using the IQR method, the boxplot shows a cleaner, more compact distribution of Maximum Wind values. It's clear that the extreme points that could distort the analysis have been reduced, improving data accuracy.

Boxplot Interpretation:¶

When comparing the first boxplots (before cleaning) with the new ones after applying the IQR method, the difference is very apparent. The original plots had many extreme points (outliers) that made the data look very spread out and harder to interpret. After removing these outliers, the boxplots became smaller and more compact, making the typical wind range more visible.

We continue to see that the higher the wind intensity (flag = 1), the lower the central pressure compared to storms with light winds (flag = 0). The outliers have been reduced (not much, but sufficiently), facilitating the interpretation of the difference between the two groups and confirming the expected inverse correlation between wind strength and atmospheric pressure.

Key Insights¶

  • Lower atmospheric pressure is generally associated with higher wind speeds, which aligns with known cyclone dynamics
  • The dataset contains significant variability in storm intensity, indicating a wide range of cyclone behaviours
  • Certain extreme values highlight the presence of very intense storm events

Conclusion¶

This project demonstrates how raw climate data can be transformed into meaningful insights through data analysis techniques.

By applying data cleaning and exploratory analysis, it was possible to identify important relationships between key atmospheric variables.

The project also highlights the importance of combining domain knowledge in meteorology with data analytics skills to better understand extreme weather events.
